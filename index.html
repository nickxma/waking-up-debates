Where Waking Up Teachers Disagree

The Waking Up app features 26+ contemplative teachers. On the questions that matter most, they land on opposite sides — and the app's own host doesn't always resolve them.

Debate 1 of 5
Does more practice lead to more benefit?
Every app, retreat center, and teacher incentive assumes the answer is yes. The Waking Up corpus reveals a split: roughly half the teachers say dosage matters, the other half say the premise is wrong. Sam Harris has no dosage model and has acknowledged losing his sense of urgency about practice over time.
Where the teachers divide
Yes — practice accumulates
Real gains come from sustained effort. The skill deepens over time. Someone with decades of daily practice has a genuine advantage.
Charlotte Joko Beck Henry Shukman Joseph Goldstein Donald Robertson Amishi Jha Rick Hanson Arthur Brooks Shinzen Young Kieran Setiya
No — you can't improve what's already complete
The thing you're looking for was never absent. A long-term practitioner has no fundamental advantage over someone encountering this for the first time.
Kodo Sawaki Adyashanti Gangaji Joan Tollifson Oliver Burkeman Richard Lang John Astin
The relationship is non-linear
Too little and nothing shifts. Too much and it can become counterproductive. Individual variation matters more than total hours. The curve isn't what the industry assumes.
Willoughby Britton Loch Kelly James Low Stephan Bodian Christopher Titmuss
There's no path — and no one to walk it
The dosage question assumes a project and a practitioner. Both of which are exactly what practice is supposed to undo. The frame is self-defeating.
Jim Newman Robert Sapolsky Peter Fenner
Sam Harris has no explicit dosage model and has acknowledged, in his own lessons, losing his sense of urgency about formal sitting practice. He is not in any of these camps — which is itself a data point.
Debate 2 of 5
Can you dissolve the neurotic self without dissolving the motivated self?
Mindfulness promises to reduce anxiety, reactivity, and ego-driven suffering. But the anxious self and the driven self may run on the same machinery. If you weaken one, do you weaken both? This is the question the Waking Up corpus never resolves — and mostly avoids.
Where the teachers divide
Yes — acceptance enables clearer action
When reactivity dissolves, what remains is more effective, not less. Genuine motivation doesn't require anxiety to function. The claim: acceptance and ambition are compatible.
Sam Harris Diane Musho Hamilton Rick Hanson Arthur Brooks Loch Kelly Stephan Bodian
No — you can't cherry-pick
The capacity for sustained effort, deferred gratification, and competitive ambition are all ego-driven. There is no surgical option for dissolving only the parts you don't like.
No teacher in the corpus directly states this position — which is the most significant finding. The argument that you can't cherry-pick is structurally present but never named.
Both selves dissolve — something else takes over
At sufficient depth, the neurotic/motivated distinction collapses. The self that was trying to cherry-pick is the very thing that dissolves. What replaces it is action without a self behind it.
Jim Newman John Astin Gangaji Joan Tollifson Adyashanti
Unresolved — no mechanism given
The question is acknowledged but left open. "Acceptance doesn't mean passivity" is asserted. What takes over for self-interested goals — career, creative achievement, competitive success — is never explained.
Sam Harris Peter Fenner
This is the structural gap in the corpus. Across ~760 hours of content, no teacher offers a mechanism for what motivates self-interested action after ego dissolution. The question is either avoided, asserted away ("compassion fills the gap"), or acknowledged and left open.
Debate 3 of 5
Are you already awakened right now?
One of the oldest debates in contemplative practice. The Waking Up corpus features teachers on every side — including at least one who calls the "you're already there" teaching outright dangerous, and another whose own awakening experience directly contradicts the "always already" position.
Where the teachers divide
Yes — right now, no practice needed
Awareness is already present, complete, undamaged. Recognition is available immediately. The entire project of gradual practice may be misdirected effort toward something you already are.
Stephan Bodian Peter Fenner Richard Lang John Astin Gangaji
Awareness is always present, but a real shift is still needed
The underlying nature is always there, but recognition requires stabilization. There's a difference between a glimpse and a stable change. The "always already" framing can mislead.
Sam Harris Adyashanti James Low Loch Kelly Shinzen Young Rick Hanson Joseph Goldstein
No — there's a real, irreversible shift with a clear before and after
Awakening is a genuine, verifiable change — not a reframing or recognition. The "you're already there" teaching may be technically true in some sense but is functionally misleading and can produce complacency rather than transformation.
Henry Shukman
The claim is problematic
Telling practitioners they're already enlightened produces nothing transformative. It tends to generate spiritual bypassing and can, in some cases, cause measurable harm.
Christopher Titmuss Willoughby Britton
Shukman's position is uniquely data-grounded: he points to his own cessation experience as evidence that awakening is a real, irreversible shift — which simultaneously undermines both the "always already there" camp and the "nowhere to get" camp.
Debate 4 of 5
Is meditation always safe?
The wellness industry treats this as obvious. But among Waking Up teachers, the split is stark: a handful acknowledge adverse effects explicitly and in detail; the majority — including Sam Harris — do not address it at all. The silence is structural, not accidental.
Where the teachers divide
Adverse effects are real and documented
The empirical evidence for adverse effects — ranging from unsettling to seriously disorienting — is robust. Individual variation in response to different techniques is real, consistent, and underdisclosed. Informed consent requires acknowledging this.
Willoughby Britton Henry Shukman Diane Musho Hamilton Loch Kelly Stephan Bodian Gangaji Rick Hanson
Individual variation matters — one size doesn't fit all
Different techniques work differently for different people. What's reliable for one practitioner can be actively unhelpful for another. The approach has to match the individual.
Willoughby Britton Shinzen Young Amishi Jha Gangaji
Not addressed
These teachers do not discuss adverse effects, limitations, or individual risk in their Waking Up content. This is not a neutral position — it shapes what practitioners expect from practice.
Sam Harris Kodo Sawaki James Low Charlotte Joko Beck Joan Tollifson Peter Fenner Joseph Goldstein Christopher Titmuss John Astin Kieran Setiya Jim Newman Donald Robertson Robert Sapolsky Oliver Burkeman
Safety depends on guidance
The same technique produces very different outcomes with skilled individual guidance vs. self-directed app-based practice. The gap between those two contexts is almost never acknowledged.
Stephan Bodian Henry Shukman Willoughby Britton
Britton is the only teacher in the corpus brought on specifically to address adverse effects. Among the 26 teachers, she is the primary — and in many cases only — voice documenting what can go wrong. The asymmetry between the depth of coverage on benefits vs. harms is itself a finding.
Debate 5 of 5
What is suffering actually for?
The Waking Up corpus contains both therapeutic teachers (mindfulness reduces suffering) and contemplative teachers (suffering is the teacher). These frameworks are in direct tension. Both claim to be what "mindfulness" is — but they point in opposite directions.
Where the teachers divide
Suffering is a necessary teacher — move toward it
Suffering is what wakes you up. The instinct to avoid discomfort is the very mechanism keeping you stuck. Moving toward it, not away, is how practice deepens. Eliminating it prematurely removes the signal.
Adyashanti Charlotte Joko Beck Henry Shukman Diane Musho Hamilton Rick Hanson Donald Robertson Kieran Setiya Arthur Brooks
Suffering is an obstacle to remove
The reduction of unnecessary suffering is the entire point of practice. Mindfulness is a tool for alleviating what doesn't serve you. The therapeutic model doesn't need a philosophical frame to work.
James Low John Astin Willoughby Britton Amishi Jha Robert Sapolsky
Suffering is something to see through
Neither a teacher nor an obstacle. Suffering is a pattern to recognize. You don't learn from it or eliminate it — you stop being fooled by it. The problem isn't the suffering, it's the relationship to it.
Joan Tollifson Gangaji Jim Newman
Suffering is a feedback signal
Suffering is information about misalignment — between behavior and values, action and consequence. It points toward what needs calibration, not transcendence. The question isn't what it's for, but what it's pointing at.
Shinzen Young
Sam Harris is not clearly in any camp here — he describes suffering as something to see through but doesn't address the question of what it's for in a sustained way. His content is largely silent on the role of suffering as a teacher.
